CHAPTER 0: The Mud Gospel
Part 1 of 3

The trenches had been dug so long they no longer looked like work. They looked like weather.

Mud walls slumped under their own weight. Plasteel revetments sagged, their rivets furred with orange corrosion. The duckboards were a floating suggestion, half-swallowed by trench water that stank of rot and propellant and old latrine pits. Overhead, the sky pulsed with distant muzzle-flashes and the slow, oily bloom of artillery impact, like bruises spreading across cloud.

They ringed the hive like a tightening noose. Somewhere beyond the wire and the crater-field, the hive’s outer hab-stacks rose in tiers, windows dead or burning, its spires spearing the low ceiling of smoke. A loyalist city on a loyalist world, and still the ground had learned to breathe wrong.

Trooper Krail kept his mask on. He kept it on even when it fogged and the lenses filmed with his own breath. He kept it on even when the seals itched his raw cheeks and the filter canister gave him that faint, chemical sweetness at the back of the tongue that meant it was working and dying at the same time.

A shell screamed in, a drawn-out metal animal sound. The trench shook. Mud and grit rattled down like hard rain.

“Head down,” Corporal Venn barked, though nobody needed telling. He was crouched in the firing step, shoulders hunched as if he could make himself smaller than his flak plates. His lasgun was wrapped in oilcloth and prayer strips, not Imperial ones anymore. The parchment had been scraped clean and re-inked with a looping sigil that looked like a child’s attempt at a smile.

Krail swallowed. The mask amplified it, made it loud inside his skull. “How long?”

Venn’s eyes were pale behind his own lenses. They flicked to the chrono nailed into the trench wall—its face cracked, its hands stuck, still useful because it reminded them time was broken. “Until it stops,” he said. Then, quieter: “Or until we do.”

The artillery never stopped. That was the new doctrine. Not tactics. Not manoeuvre. Just subtraction. Feed the guns. Feed the line. Feed the mud.

A runner slid down the trench bank, boots slipping, catching himself with a gloved hand that came up brown-black. He shoved a wax-stamped slate into Venn’s chest plate.

“Orders,” the runner panted. His mask had a tear at the cheek; a rag was tied over it, wet with something dark. “From… from the rear.”

Venn looked at the seal first. Not the ink. Not the words. The seal.

It wasn’t the Aquila. It wasn’t any Munitorum mark. It was a stamped circle with tally-notches cut through it, as if the wax itself had been bitten. Three notches. Then four. Then seven. A count that meant nothing until it meant everything.

Krail watched Venn’s fingers tremble as he broke it. The corporal read fast, lips moving behind the respirator.

“What?” Krail asked.

Venn didn’t answer immediately. His gaze went down the trench, to where the men huddled in twos and threes under sheets of camo-net and ripped canvas. To where the medicae corner had been set up beside the sump drain, because the drain was already a wound and wounds belonged together. To where a pair of bodies lay under a tarp, their boots sticking out like accusations.

Venn finally spoke. “Expenditure limits,” he said. “And… collection.”

Krail felt his stomach clench. “Collection of what?”

Venn’s eyes went flat. “You know what.”

A cough started somewhere behind them. It wasn’t a normal cough. It was wet and thick, the kind that came up from deep and brought something with it. A chorus answered—mask-coughs, suppressed because coughing was rationed now too. Every cough meant a seal check. Every seal check meant you learned which men were running out of luck.

A voice drifted along the trench, low and rhythmic. Not shouted. Not preached. Just breathed.

“Grandfather keeps. Grandfather counts. Grandfather knows.”

The words moved from mouth to mouth like a shared infection. Men repeated them without looking at each other, because looking made it real.

Krail’s fingers tightened around his lasgun. The grip was slick with trench water. He could feel the tiny vibrations of distant impacts through the stock. “This is madness,” he said, and hated himself for saying it out loud.

Venn’s head snapped toward him. “Don’t,” he hissed. “Not here.”

Krail glanced up the trench. A commissar stood two bays down, half in shadow beneath a corrugated overhang. He still wore the old coat, black once, now the colour of drowned cloth. The cap brim was stained with mildew. He wasn’t looking at the bombardment. He was looking at them.

Even through lenses, the commissar’s gaze had weight. It made the air inside Krail’s mask feel thinner.

Venn lowered his voice further. “First battle under the new… understanding,” he said. He couldn’t bring himself to say *faith*. It tasted like betrayal. “You want to be the first to name it wrong?”

Krail stared at the wax crumbs on Venn’s gauntlet. The tally-seal had left a greasy smear. It looked like something had pressed its thumb into their orders and left rot behind.

A shell landed close enough that the trench wall bowed inward. Mud sloughed down, burying a man up to his waist. He screamed and then choked as thick water filled his mouth. Hands grabbed, hauled—too late. The man’s mask came off in the struggle. For a moment, his face was bare in the poisonous air.

He inhaled once.

Krail saw the instant the air changed him. His cheeks puffed. His eyes went wide, then soft. Blood ran from his nose in a slow, almost gentle line. He reached for something—his rifle, his friend, the dirt—then his hand fell.

Silence followed, heavy as sand.

Then the low prayer started again, as if the line couldn’t survive without noise.

“Grandfather keeps. Grandfather counts. Grandfather knows.”

Venn stared at the dead man’s face. His voice was rough. “Filters,” he muttered, and Krail didn’t know if it was a curse or a tally.

A medicae servitor clanked down the trench with a stretcher frame. Someone had bolted a rusted icon to its chest plate—three circles, pitted and leaking a greasy moisture. The servitor’s augmetic saw whined once, eager, then it moved on.

Krail felt bile rise. The mask made it worse, trapping the smell of his own fear.

Venn slapped the slate against the trench wall with a wet sound. “Listen,” he said, forcing his tone into something like command. “We’re not the Astra anymore. You want to pretend we are, you’ll die like that—alone, naked, choking on air that doesn’t care what you *meant*.”

Krail’s mouth went dry. “So what are we?”

Venn hesitated. Just a heartbeat. Long enough that Krail saw something human behind the corporal’s discipline—revulsion, relief, hunger, all braided together.

“We’re what’s left,” Venn said. “And what’s left gets used.”

The runner leaned in, whispering through his ragged mask. “They’re calling for the count by dusk. Bodies. Numbers. No mistakes.”

Krail’s gaze flicked again to the commissar. The man’s gloved hand rested on his pistol grip, casual, patient. Like he was waiting for an argument the way a butcher waited for a blade to slip.

“Numbers,” Krail echoed.

Venn tapped the slate with two fingers. “Ammo allotment is down. Rations are down. Fuel’s down. Everything’s down.” He swallowed. “But the tally is up.”

Another distant impact, closer. The trench lamps guttered. For a second, the darkness breathed in.

When the lights steadied, Krail noticed something he hadn’t wanted to see before: along the trench wall, hammered into the plasteel braces, someone had fixed a strip of parchment with a neat row of notches inked along it. A ledger, but not for supply.

Names were there. Some crossed out. Some not yet. Beside each name, a number.

Krail’s throat tightened. “Whose is that?”

Venn didn’t look at it. “Don’t touch it.”

“But—”

“Don’t. Touch it.” Venn’s voice cut like wire. “You touch that ledger, you invite the count to notice you.”

Krail’s hands shook. He forced them still. He stared past the ledger, past the mud, past the wire, to the hive in the distance.

The hive’s guns answered their own, a loyalist counter-barrage that made the air scream. Somewhere out there were men in clean uniforms and uncorrupted prayers, believing the Emperor still listened.

Krail tried to remember what that felt like.

Behind him, the trench prayer thickened, a low tide rising.

“Grandfather keeps. Grandfather counts. Grandfather knows.”

And on the slate against the wall, the wax-seal’s notches seemed to grin as the bombardment rolled on.
